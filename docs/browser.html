Browser Wars

In terms of technology that has proven to be groundbreaking amongst human civilization, there in recent years has been an invention that seemed to have come out of nowhere. When thinking of the pantheon of intuitive technology that changed the way humans function daily, the world wide web was not always the most regarded. Inventions such as the aeroplane, light bulb, steam engine were once heavily dominated when mentioning new technologies. The age of the internet began with a huge clash for power with the purpose of determining who would control the future of the newly forming web, ending in a courtroom battle and becoming known as the age of the browser war.

The World Wide Web was invented in 1989 by a Mister Tim Berners-Lee, an English scientist. At the time the web was very much unlike how it is today and was very barebones, no where close to being a mouse-click away from millions of people, music, and information. There was however line after line after line of text, and you had to be “geeky” to be enthusiastic about such a thing as mentioned in the video.

One of these so called “geeks” was a student from the University of Illinois, Marc Andreesen who had the idea that “regular” people would soon use the internet as we do today, on such a normal scale it is now engraved in our daily lives. Anderson with this dream in mind set out to make the web more user friendly, adding media such as pictures and audio and their most important work, the point and click capabilities that are present in today's graphical web design. They went to work to make a mosaic but before this mosaic could be mass produced, they were in desperate need of cash flow.

Jim Clark, a self made man who studied computer science was intrigued by this mosaic, so he contacted Marc and gave him a job in Silicon Valley for one of his Clarks companies. Bill Gates who is the CEO and founder of the monopoly over PC operating systems and with such a tight grip on the computer industry. By 1993 about 90 percent of the world's PCs were running Microsoft, this brought competitors to their knees including the likes of IBM. Under the likes of Jim Clark, pioneers of the industry would soon launch the fastest growing software company the world had ever seen, Netscape Communications.

Due to the lightning quick emergence of this company, it caught the attention of other powerhouses such as the overwhelming force of Microsoft. Immediately the thought process was how can we work with Netscape in order to bolster the company further, such as “How can Microsoft Word work with Netscape.” This soon took a turn for the worst with Microsoft attempting to buy out Netscape for 1 million dollars and any refusal of such would result in the copying of Netscape's features. This sparked a lawsuit against Microsoft and Netscape would want to go public as this was only their first year and they had no money. With the launch of IPO, Netscape's stock went off the charts which then ignited the boom that would soon rage for a decade.

Microsoft went on to indulge in their own version of browser as they wanted a piece of the action as well. Internet explorer as we know is a powerhouse, and Clark finally felt the pressure of Microsoft breathing down its neck. Microsoft had astonishingly more money than Netscape and therefore could allow free use of the browser which marked the downfall of Netscape by 1997.
